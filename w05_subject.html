Collision Theory

Collision Theory describes how and why molecules would react on the atomic level. In order for a molecule to react with another it needs: correct orientation and enough kinetic energy to move an atom. For most collisions, they either have the wrong orientation (because they need to hit the right atom and the right spot) or not enough kinetic energy (particularly if the temperature is low). As shown in the graph, the amount of kinetic energy can be modeled by the activation energy (which is the hump in the graph). In general, if we were to increase the temperature by 10 degrees Celsius, the reaction rate would double.

Collision theory explanation

Another way that the rate of reaction could be changed is using a catalyst. The definition of a catalyst is a substance that provides an alternate pathway for the reaction without being consumed in the reaction. It decreases the activation energy of the reaction. The opposites of catalysts are inhibitors, which slow down a reaction by increasing the activation energy. They can be categorized into 2 main categories: homogenous catalysts (which are the same state as the reactant) and hetergenous (which are different)

Rate Laws

For a general reaction, the rate of reaction (how fast the molecules react) is equal to the rate constant * the reactant to a certain power. Most reactions occur in multiple steps because having multiple molecules collide all at once are very rate. Thus, as seen by this reaction, there are 2 molecules colliding at one time. To determine the rate law for the overall reaction, we look at the elementary slow step of the reaction. The rate law would be k (rate constant) * reactant raised to the power of their coefficient. For example, if a reactant has a coefficient of 1, then it would be the k * reactant. To find the rate law, we look at the elementary slow step and use that for the rate formula (k * reactant to the coefficient).

The main three types of reactions are zero order reactions, first order reactions, and second order reactions. By zero, first, and second, we refer to the sum of the exponents of the rate law. For each of the respective reactions, there is a linear equation that relates the concentrations of the reactant to time. If we were to have the concentration in respect to time, we can plot each of these. There is also a specific half-life equation, which tells you the half-life (how long it takes for a substance to decrease to half its size) based on the rate constant. Rate Constant

We have seen previously in the rate laws that k appears frequently. Well, to calculate it we use the Arrhenius equation. According to it, k = A * e^(-Ea/RT). Let's break it down. The first term, A, the Arrhenius factor, just says the probability that any molecule will have the correct orientation when colliding. If A were to increase, k would also increase. This makes sense because if more molecules have the correct orientation, they will react (if they have enough energy). That is the first part that needs to happen in order for a molecule to react: orientation. The next term deals with the other factor: temperature. e^(-Ea/RT) is the probability that a molecule will have enough kinetic energy. Ea is the activation energy. R is the ideal gas constant (8.314 J/mol * K). T is the temperature in Kelvin. Let's see how k changes if we change the Ea and T. If Ea increases, then k would decrease (since the exponent is negated and it is in the numerator). This makes sense because if the molecules have a larger hump, less can successfully react. If T increases, then k increases. This also makes sense because then more molecules have a higher kinetic energy, leading to more effective collisions.
