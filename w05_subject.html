Collision Theory

In order for two molecules to react, they must have correct orientation and collide with sufficient kinetic energy. However, most don't react since they don't have enough energy or aren't in the right orientation.

Collision Theory explained

In order to find out how much energy is needed for the atoms to collide, you look at the activation energy (Ea). The ways to change the Ea are: changing the temperature, prescence of a catalyst, and changing the surface area. On average for any reaction, an increase of temperature 10 degrees Celsius doubles the reaction rate. Catalysts and inhibitors change the pathway of the reaction (by making the molecules do something else but collide together), thereby changing the activation energy. Catalysts can be categorized into 2 subcategories: homogenous catalysts (meaning they are in the same physical state as the molecules present) or heterogeneous catalysts (they are in different states).

Rate Laws

For a general reaction, the rate of reaction (how fast the molecules react) is equal to the rate constant * the reactant to a certain power. Most reactions occur in multiple steps because having multiple molecules collide all at once are very rate. Thus, as seen by this reaction, there are 2 molecules colliding at one time. To determine the rate law for the overall reaction, we look at the elementary slow step of the reaction. The rate law would be k (rate constant) * reactant raised to the power of their coefficient. For example, if a reactant has a coefficient of 1, then it would be the k * reactant.

The main three types of reactions are zero order reactions, first order reactions, and second order reactions. By zero, first, and second, we refer to the sum of the exponents of the rate law. For each of the different reaction orders, there is a respective linear equation that shows the relationship between the concentration and time on a graph. Also, there is an equation for the half-life of the reaction (which is the amount of time it takes for half of a given substance to react.
